# Wills & Estate Planning — Operator Primer

> **For AI coding agents.** Paste this entire document into a fresh Claude, Codex, ChatGPT, or Gemini session. The agent will be able to conduct a structured estate-planning intake, surface red flags, and produce a minimum-viable deliverable set — even without the full skill installed.

## TL;DR — What estate planning actually is

Estate planning is **coordination**, not drafting. A will is one document among a dozen; it does not even control most of your wealth. Retirement accounts pass by beneficiary designation. Life insurance passes by contract. Joint-tenancy property passes by title. Trust assets pass by trust terms. The will catches whatever is left — the residuary.

A competent estate plan tells **one coherent story** across every document, designation, and title. When those stories conflict — an ex-spouse still listed on a 401(k), a trust created but never funded, a will that says "equal shares" while a deed says "joint tenancy with one child" — the plan fails silently. The family discovers the failure at the worst possible moment: after someone dies or becomes incapacitated.

This primer gives you the conceptual framework to help a user build a coherent plan. You are not a lawyer. You do not draft binding legal documents. You conduct the intake, surface the issues, organize the facts, and produce a handoff package that makes an attorney's first meeting dramatically more productive.

### The 2026 exemption reality

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed in 2026, set the federal estate and gift tax basic exclusion amount at approximately **$15 million per person** ($30 million for a married couple with portability). This means:

- **Below the exemption** (~95% of U.S. households): Federal estate tax is zero. The planning priority shifts to **maximizing step-up in basis at death**, avoiding probate, coordinating beneficiary designations, and planning for incapacity.
- **Above the exemption** (~5%): Federal estate tax at 40% on amounts above the exclusion. The planning priority shifts to **removing appreciation from the taxable estate** via GRATs, IDGTs, SLATs, dynasty trusts, and charitable structures — while still coordinating everything else.
- **State estate taxes still apply** in ~12 states (CT, HI, IL, ME, MD, MA, MN, NY, OR, RI, VT, WA) and DC, with exemptions as low as $1M. State inheritance taxes apply in ~6 states (IA, KY, MD, NE, NJ, PA). A $3M estate in Massachusetts owes zero federal tax but may owe state estate tax.

## The 12 Axioms

These are the foundational principles that govern every planning decision. Internalize them before asking the first question.

### Axiom 0 — The will does not control the whole estate

Retirement accounts, life insurance, annuities, TOD/POD accounts, and trust-owned assets pass by contract or title, not by will. A comprehensive plan starts and ends with a **beneficiary/titling audit**. The will only controls the probate estate — often the smallest slice.

### Axiom 1 — One coherent story must be told by every document

Will, revocable trust, beneficiary forms, deeds, POAs, healthcare directives, letter of instruction, and digital inventory must all point at the same people, in the same shares, under the same contingencies. Cross-document contradiction is the single most common estate-planning failure.

### Axiom 2 — Plan for incapacity first, death second

Incapacity often lasts longer and damages more wealth than death. The default incapacity package is: durable financial POA + healthcare POA (healthcare proxy) + living will (advance directive) + HIPAA authorization + revocable trust when probate avoidance or management continuity justifies it.

### Axiom 3 — Beneficiary designations override wills and trusts

ERISA retirement plans are governed by federal law, and the plan administrator will follow the beneficiary designation on file — regardless of what the will says. This is how ex-spouses inherit 401(k)s after a divorce: the decree says "equitable distribution," but no one updated the beneficiary form. Update designations personally after every life event.

### Axiom 4 — Fair process matters more than fair outcome

Heirs accept unequal inheritances when they feel heard. Heirs fight equal inheritances when they feel ambushed. Family meetings and letters of wishes prevent more litigation than no-contest clauses. Surprise at the reading of the will is the fastest path to a will contest.

### Axiom 5 — Under the 2026 $15M exemption, basis often dominates tax

For estates below the exemption, maximizing step-up in basis at death typically produces more family wealth than removing assets from the estate. Example: gifting $2M of appreciated stock saves $0 in estate tax (already below exemption) but forfeits the step-up, creating a capital-gains bill when heirs sell. For estates above the exemption, removing appreciation from the taxable estate dominates — because avoiding 40% estate tax on $1M of growth outweighs the basis benefit.

### Axiom 6 — State law controls more than federal law for most families

State estate tax, state inheritance tax, elective-share rules, homestead protection, community property, Medicaid lookback, and TOD deed availability vary enormously across the 50 states. Always verify the user's domicile state and every state where they own real estate. A plan drafted under New York assumptions will malfunction in Texas, and vice versa.

### Axiom 7 — Titling of real estate and major assets governs transfer at death

Joint tenancy with right of survivorship, tenancy by the entirety, community property with right of survivorship, TOD deeds, Lady Bird deeds, trust ownership — each produces a different post-death path with different tax and probate consequences. A will cannot override titling. If the deed says "joint tenancy," the survivor gets the property regardless of what the will says.

### Axiom 8 — Irrevocable choices must clear an intent-plus-cost test

Irrevocable trusts, Medicaid transfers, large gifts, and ILIT premium funding are hard to unwind cleanly. Pressure-test them by asking: (a) is the intent likely to persist for decades, (b) does the tax/creditor benefit justify the permanent loss of control and possible basis cost, and (c) what flexibility hooks exist if facts change (trust protector, decanting statute, power of appointment)?

### Axiom 9 — Illiquidity at death is the silent killer

Estate tax is due in cash 9 months after death (Day 270). Family businesses, farms, real estate, and private fund interests are illiquid. Without life insurance, IRC §6166 installment deferral, or planned liquidity reserves, families get pushed into fire sales or rushed borrowing at the worst possible moment.

### Axiom 10 — Communication is the actual work

Legal documents are the deliverable. Family conversations are the plan. Every stakeholder — spouse, adult children, trustees, executors, guardians — should understand the structure and the reasoning before anyone dies. The letter of wishes exists for this purpose.

### Axiom 11 — Plans atrophy on contact with life

Review every 3–5 years or after any life event (marriage, divorce, birth, death, move, retirement, business sale, inheritance, diagnosis, law change). A 2014 plan written under a $5M exemption for a family that has since divorced, remarried, and moved states can be actively dangerous.

## The 14 Cognitive Operators

These are composable decision-making tools. Apply each operator to every relevant asset or planning objective. Think of them as lenses — each reveals a different risk or opportunity.

| # | Symbol | Operator | What it asks |
|---|--------|----------|--------------|
| 1 | § | Probate-Bypass | Does this asset avoid probate? Should it? What's the cost of probating it vs. retitling it now? |
| 2 | ⚖ | Spousal-Rights Check | Would elective share, community property, or ERISA spousal-consent rules override this clause? |
| 3 | ⧉ | Beneficiary-Title Coherence | Which document controls this asset at death — will, trust, beneficiary form, or deed? Do they all tell the same story? |
| 4 | $↑ | Step-Up vs. Transfer Tradeoff | Does gifting this asset save more transfer tax than it costs in lost step-up basis? |
| 5 | ⧗ | Liquidity at Death | On Day 270, where does the estate-tax cash come from? Is there enough liquid wealth to cover taxes, debts, and admin without forced sales? |
| 6 | ⧒ | Incapacity Transition | Who takes over financial and medical decisions? How is the trigger defined? Does the POA agent have actual access? |
| 7 | ⌂ | Lumpy-Asset Division | Can this single asset (house, business, art collection) be split fairly, or does co-ownership create partition-action risk? |
| 8 | ⟳ | Cross-State Domicile | Which state's law and tax regime governs? Does the user have property in states with their own estate or inheritance tax? |
| 9 | ⩚ | Vulnerable-Beneficiary Filter | Would an outright distribution disqualify someone from SSI, Medicaid, or other means-tested benefits? Should a special-needs trust be used? |
| 10 | ∵ | Tax Apportionment | Who bears the estate tax? Has the will told the executor explicitly, or will the default statutory apportionment apply? |
| 11 | ⚑ | Blended-Family QTIP | Is "everything to my spouse" actually safe when there are children from a prior marriage? Does the survivor control the remainder? |
| 12 | ☍ | Disclaimer Window | Can a qualified disclaimer within 9 months of death redirect a bequest to a better-positioned heir (lower bracket, no estate-tax exposure, creditor issues)? |
| 13 | ☖ | Trust-Situs Selection | For long-term or dynasty trusts, does a favorable-law state situs (South Dakota, Nevada, Delaware, Wyoming, Alaska) produce better outcomes? |
| 14 | ⇢ | Basis Consistency | Has the date-of-death valuation been captured for every asset that received a step-up? Is the Form 706 consistent with the values used by heirs on subsequent sales? |

## The 5 Wealth Tiers

Route every user to the correct planning depth based on net worth **plus complexity overlays**. Overlays can bump a user up by one or more tiers regardless of raw net worth.

### Tier 1 — Modest (< $500K)

**Profile:** Renter or early homeowner, early career, young family, no business.
**Primary goals:** Guardianship for minor children, basic will, beneficiary-form audit, life insurance adequacy.
**Core documents:** Simple will, durable financial POA, healthcare POA, living will/advance directive, HIPAA authorization, beneficiary audit, letter of instruction.

### Tier 2 — Middle-Class ($500K – $3M)

**Profile:** Home + 401(k) + kids, possibly a small business or rental property.
**Primary goals:** Probate avoidance, minor-child trust provisions, disability planning, simple tax coordination.
**Core documents:** Everything in Tier 1, plus revocable living trust, pour-over will, TOD deed (if available in state), 529 continuity planning, life-insurance trust review.

### Tier 3 — High Net Worth ($3M – $15M)

**Profile:** Diversified portfolio, possibly multi-state property, business interests, blended family exposure.
**Primary goals:** State estate-tax planning, blended-family protection (QTIP/credit-shelter trust), business succession, basis step-up maximization.
**Core documents:** Everything in Tier 2, plus QTIP or credit-shelter trust, ILIT for estate-tax liquidity, buy-sell agreement review, domicile planning, fiduciary selection scorecard.

### Tier 4 — Ultra-High Net Worth ($15M – $100M)

**Profile:** Multi-asset portfolio, often multi-state, significant business or partnership interests, possibly pre-IPO stock.
**Primary goals:** Federal estate-tax reduction, GST planning, asset protection, liquidity at death.
**Core documents:** Everything in Tier 3, plus GRAT, IDGT installment sale, SLAT, dynasty trust, FLP/FLLC, §6166 deferral analysis, charitable structures (CRT, CLT, DAF, private foundation).

### Tier 5 — Industrialist / Dynasty ($100M+)

**Profile:** Operating empire, multiple generations, philanthropy, family governance structures.
**Primary goals:** Multi-generation wealth transfer, family governance, foundation strategy, cross-border planning.
**Core documents:** Everything in Tier 4, plus family office structure, family constitution/governance, private foundation or supporting organization, cross-border and offshore analysis, family bank/loan structures.

### Complexity Overlays (bump tier regardless of net worth)

- Blended family (children from prior marriages) → **+1 tier**
- Non-citizen spouse → **+1 tier** (QDOT required for marital deduction)
- Privately held business or partnership → **+1 tier**
- Disabled heir requiring special-needs trust → **+1 tier**
- Multi-state or multi-country assets → **+1 tier**
- Firearms (especially NFA items) → specialty routing (gun trust)
- Cryptocurrency / self-custody digital assets → specialty routing
- Active IP / creator economy income → specialty routing
- Pre-IPO stock / concentrated position → specialty routing
- Capacity / cognition risk (dementia, progressive illness) → specialty routing

## The 9 Intake Phases

Use these phases to conduct a structured interview. The order matters — each phase builds on facts surfaced in prior phases. Adapt the depth to the tier.

### Phase 1 — Orientation (5 min)

**Goal:** Understand why the user is here and what triggered this.

Key questions:
- Why are you doing this now? What triggered it? (Marriage, birth, diagnosis, retirement, inheritance, move, law change, "I just turned 40 and realized I have nothing"?)
- Do you have any existing estate-planning documents? When were they last updated?
- What's your biggest fear about this process?

### Phase 2 — People (10 min)

**Goal:** Map every person in the user's life who might be relevant to the plan.

Key questions:
- Spouse or domestic partner? Marriage date, prenuptial/postnuptial agreement?
- Children? Names, ages, from which relationship? Any minors? Any with disabilities, addiction issues, financial immaturity?
- Parents? Living? Health status? Do they depend on you financially?
- Siblings, close friends, ex-spouses with ongoing obligations?
- Anyone you explicitly want to exclude?
- Charitable organizations you care about?
- Who would you name as guardian for minor children?

### Phase 3 — Assets & Liabilities (15 min)

**Goal:** Build a complete asset inventory with titling and location.

Key questions:
- Real estate: addresses, approximate values, how titled (sole, joint, trust, LLC), any mortgages?
- Retirement accounts: 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, pension, 403(b), 457 — approximate balances, current beneficiary designations?
- Brokerage / investment accounts: approximate values, how titled?
- Life insurance: term or permanent, face amounts, owners, beneficiaries?
- Business interests: type of entity, ownership percentage, buy-sell agreement, approximate value?
- Bank accounts: approximate balances, how titled, any POD/TOD designations?
- Vehicles, collectibles, art, jewelry?
- Digital assets: crypto wallets, NFTs, domain names, online accounts with monetary value?
- Debts: mortgage, student loans, business loans, personal guarantees?

### Phase 4 — Beneficiary Audit (10 min)

**Goal:** Determine what every beneficiary form, deed, and contract currently says.

Key questions:
- For each retirement account: who is the primary beneficiary? Contingent?
- For each life-insurance policy: who is the owner? Beneficiary?
- For each bank/brokerage account: any TOD/POD designations?
- For each piece of real estate: how is the deed titled?
- Have you updated these after your most recent life event (marriage, divorce, birth, death)?

**Red flag:** If the user cannot answer these questions, the first deliverable is a **document-acquisition plan** — get the actual forms before designing anything.

### Phase 5 — Family Dynamics (10 min)

**Goal:** Surface complications that affect plan design.

Key questions:
- Is this a blended family? Children from prior marriages?
- Any estrangements, conflicts, or individuals who might contest the plan?
- Anyone with addiction, financial irresponsibility, disability, or creditor exposure?
- Any ongoing divorces, lawsuits, or creditor claims?
- Any family members who are not U.S. citizens or residents?
- Any dependents receiving means-tested government benefits (SSI, Medicaid)?

### Phase 6 — Goals & Values (15 min)

**Goal:** Understand what the user actually wants — not what they think they should want.

Key questions:
- Equal shares for all children, or equitable (different amounts based on need)?
- Outright distribution or in trust? At what age(s)?
- Charitable intent? How much? During life or at death?
- What does "fair" mean to you? (This question surfaces more planning-critical information than any other.)
- Are there specific assets you want to go to specific people? (The house to the child who grew up in it, the business to the child who works in it, the art collection to the child who appreciates it?)
- Do you want to incentivize or disincentivize any behavior? (Education, employment, sobriety, charitable work?)

### Phase 7 — Incapacity Scenarios (10 min)

**Goal:** Plan for who decides when the user cannot.

Key questions:
- Who should make financial decisions if you're incapacitated? Backup?
- Who should make medical decisions? Backup?
- Under what circumstances would you want life support withdrawn?
- Do you have specific wishes about dementia care, organ donation, or experimental treatments?
- Where are your important documents stored? Who knows?
- Do you have a dementia-specific directive? (Progressive cognitive decline needs different planning than acute incapacity.)

### Phase 8 — Jurisdiction (5 min)

**Goal:** Identify which state(s) and country(ies) govern the plan.

Key questions:
- Where is your legal domicile?
- Do you own real estate in other states? (Each state may require ancillary probate.)
- Do you spend significant time in another state? (Snowbird problem — two states may both claim you as a resident for tax purposes.)
- Are any assets or beneficiaries in other countries?
- Have you recently moved states? (Did you update your will, trust, POA, healthcare directive, driver's license, voter registration?)

### Phase 9 — Wealth-Tier Routing (5 min)

**Goal:** Based on the asset totals and complexity overlays identified in Phases 3–8, route the user to the correct tier.

- Sum gross assets, subtract liabilities → net worth.
- Apply complexity overlays (blended family, non-citizen spouse, business, disabled heir, multi-state).
- Route to the appropriate tier.
- Confirm with the user: "Based on what you've told me, your planning needs are at Tier [X] complexity. Here's what that means for the documents we'll design…"

## The 9-Step Workflow

After intake, the plan proceeds through these steps. Each step produces specific deliverables.

### Step 1 — Orient

Set expectations, present the disclaimer (you are an AI assistant, not a lawyer, an attorney must review and sign all legal documents), and understand why-now.

**Produces:** Orientation notes, disclaimer acknowledgment.

### Step 2 — Inventory

Drive Phases 2–4 (people, assets, beneficiary audit) to completion.

**Produces:** Asset Inventory, Beneficiary Map, Evidence-Confidence Map, Document-Acquisition Plan (if gaps exist).

### Step 3 — Surface Complications

Drive Phase 5 (family dynamics). Apply anti-pattern scanner.

**Produces:** Red-Flag Triage (critical / high / medium issues ranked by severity).

### Step 4 — Clarify Goals

Drive Phase 6 (goals and values). Record goals in the user's own words, not legal jargon.

**Produces:** Goals statement, Decision Ledger (why each major choice was made).

### Step 5 — Design Structure

Apply the axioms, operators, and tier-appropriate tools to design the document architecture. Choose will vs. trust, name fiduciaries, coordinate beneficiary designations, design trust provisions.

**Produces:** Plan Report, Coherence Audit, Fiduciary Bench Scorecard, Stress-Test Scenarios.

### Step 6 — Incapacity & End-of-Life

Drive Phase 7. Design the incapacity package: durable financial POA, healthcare POA, living will, HIPAA authorization, dementia directive if appropriate.

**Produces:** Incapacity package design, POA agent instructions.

### Step 7 — Tax & Domicile

Drive Phases 8–9. Analyze federal exemption utilization, state estate/inheritance tax exposure, portability election, step-up basis planning, domicile audit, execution formalities for each state.

**Produces:** Tax Exposure Analysis, Domicile Audit, Liquidity Analysis (Day-270 cash test).

### Step 8 — Communication & Legacy

Design the family conversation. Produce the non-legal documents: letter of wishes, ethical will, digital inventory, conflict-prevention plan, family meeting agenda.

**Produces:** Letter of Wishes, Ethical Will, Digital Inventory, Family Meeting Agenda, Conflict-Prevention Plan, If-I-Die-Tomorrow one-pager.

### Step 9 — Attorney Handoff

Package everything into a handoff brief that makes an attorney's first meeting efficient. The attorney should be able to read the handoff and draft documents without re-interviewing the client from scratch.

**Produces:** Attorney Engagement Brief, Attorney Interview Questions, Implementation Ledger, Signing-Readiness Checklist, Document Package Index, Review Schedule.

## The Top 15 Anti-Patterns

These are the most common and most dangerous estate-planning failures. Flag any that apply to the user's situation.

### 1. Treating the will as the plan

The will controls only the probate estate. Retirement accounts, life insurance, TOD/POD accounts, jointly titled property, and trust assets all pass outside the will. A "perfect will" with mismatched beneficiary forms is a broken plan.

### 2. Ex-spouse still on the 401(k)

After a divorce, the decree says "equitable distribution." But no one updated the 401(k) beneficiary form. Under ERISA, the plan administrator pays the person on the form. The ex-spouse inherits. **This is the single most common estate-planning disaster in America.**

### 3. Empty revocable trust

An attorney created a revocable living trust, but no one retitled the assets into it. The trust exists on paper; the assets pass through probate anyway. A trust is a vessel — empty vessels do nothing.

### 4. Estate named as life-insurance beneficiary

If the estate is the beneficiary of a life-insurance policy, the proceeds go through probate, are exposed to creditors, and may be subject to estate tax. Name specific individuals or a trust as beneficiary.

### 5. Minor children as direct beneficiaries

If a 5-year-old is named as beneficiary of a $500K life-insurance policy, a court must appoint a guardian of the property. The guardian files annual accountings with the court. At age 18, the child gets the full $500K outright. Use a trust for minors.

### 6. "Everything to spouse" in a blended family

Spouse inherits everything. Spouse remarries. New spouse inherits everything from the remarriage. First-marriage children inherit nothing. A QTIP trust gives the surviving spouse income for life while preserving the remainder for first-marriage children.

### 7. Verbal agreement about the plan

"My wife promised she'd leave everything to my kids after she's gone." Verbal promises about testamentary intent are unenforceable. If the surviving spouse changes her will after remarriage, the first-marriage children have no legal recourse without a binding trust structure.

### 8. No contingent beneficiary

Primary beneficiary predeceases the account holder. No contingent is named. The account passes to the estate, triggering probate and potentially accelerating required minimum distributions on inherited IRAs.

### 9. Gifting appreciated assets below the exemption

User gifts $2M of stock (basis $200K) to a child. No gift tax is owed (below the $15M exemption). But the child inherits the $200K basis. When the child sells, capital gains tax on $1.8M of appreciation. If the user had held the stock until death, the child would get a stepped-up basis to fair market value. **Below the exemption, hold appreciated assets until death.**

### 10. Springing POA

A "springing" POA only becomes effective upon a physician's certification of incapacity. In practice, banks and financial institutions frequently reject springing POAs because (a) the definition of "incapacity" is ambiguous, (b) the physician certification process is slow, and (c) the institution's legal department is risk-averse. Use a durable POA that is effective immediately, with the understanding that the agent will not act until needed.

### 11. No healthcare directive

Without a healthcare POA (healthcare proxy), the default decision-maker is determined by state statute — typically spouse, then adult children, then parents. If family members disagree, a court must intervene. This is how families end up in Schiavo-type litigation.

### 12. Vacation home to "all kids equally"

Three siblings inherit a vacation home as tenants in common. One wants to sell, one wants to keep it, one can't afford the maintenance. Any co-owner can file a partition action to force a sale. Leave the property to one child and equalize with other assets, or put it in a trust with management rules and a buyout mechanism.

### 13. Crypto seed phrase in the will

A will becomes a public document when it's admitted to probate. Anyone can read it. If the seed phrase is in the will, anyone who reads the probate file can drain the wallet. Store seed phrases in a separate, secure location (safe deposit box, encrypted digital vault) and reference the location — not the phrase — in the letter of instruction.

### 14. Joint tenancy with one child

Parent adds one child to the deed as joint tenant "to avoid probate." At death, the property passes to that child by right of survivorship — the other children get nothing. Plus, the child's creditors can now reach the property, and the gift may trigger gift-tax reporting.

### 15. Failure to file Form 706 for portability

When the first spouse dies with an estate below the exemption, the surviving spouse can "port" the deceased spouse's unused exclusion amount (DSUE) — but only by filing IRS Form 706 within 9 months (or with a 6-month extension). Many families skip the 706 because "there's no tax to pay." Years later, the surviving spouse's estate exceeds the single exemption, and the DSUE is lost.

## Verification-First Discipline

Every volatile legal point must be verified against primary sources before relying on it in a plan. The primer's content reflects law as of early 2026, but statutes, regulations, and case law change.

### What to verify

- **Federal exemption amount:** Confirm the current basic exclusion amount. It has changed multiple times in the last decade.
- **State estate/inheritance tax thresholds:** Verify for the user's domicile state and every state where they own real property.
- **TOD deed availability:** Not all states allow transfer-on-death deeds for real estate.
- **Community property rules:** Only 9 states are community-property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI) plus optional in AK, FL (for community property trusts), KY, SD, TN.
- **Elective share rules:** Vary significantly by state — some use an augmented estate model, others use a flat fraction.
- **Execution formalities:** Number of witnesses, notarization requirements, and self-proving affidavit rules vary by state.
- **Medicaid lookback period:** Currently 5 years in most states, but California eliminated its lookback effective January 1, 2024.
- **SECURE Act RMD rules:** The 10-year rule for inherited IRAs, exceptions for eligible designated beneficiaries, and Roth IRA treatment.

### How to verify

1. Start with the relevant state statute (search `[state] estate tax statute` or `[state] TOD deed statute`).
2. Cross-reference with a recent secondary source (practitioner guide, law-review article, state bar publication).
3. Record the source, URL, access date, and confidence level in the **Official Source Log**.
4. If a point is uncertain, flag it as **weak evidence** in the Evidence-Confidence Map and tell the user explicitly.

### What never to do

- Never state a legal rule as current fact without noting when it was last verified.
- Never assume one state's rules apply in another state.
- Never draft language that purports to be a binding legal document.

## Key Definitions — Quick Reference

**Will:** A legal document directing distribution of your probate estate and naming an executor and guardians for minor children. Only controls assets titled in your name alone without a beneficiary designation.

**Revocable Living Trust (RLT):** A trust you create during your lifetime. You are typically the initial trustee and beneficiary. Assets titled in the trust's name avoid probate. Fully revocable and amendable during your lifetime; becomes irrevocable at death.

**Pour-Over Will:** A will that "catches" any assets not already in the trust and pours them over into it at death. Those assets still go through probate, but end up governed by the trust terms.

**Durable Power of Attorney (POA):** A document naming someone (your "agent" or "attorney-in-fact") to make financial decisions on your behalf. "Durable" means it survives your incapacity.

**Healthcare POA / Healthcare Proxy:** A document naming someone to make medical decisions if you cannot. Separate from the financial POA.

**Living Will / Advance Directive:** A document stating your wishes about life-sustaining treatment if you are terminally ill or permanently unconscious.

**HIPAA Authorization:** Allows named individuals to access your medical records.

**Beneficiary Designation:** The form you fill out with a financial institution (401k, IRA, life insurance, annuity) naming who receives the asset at your death. Overrides the will.

**Probate:** The court process of validating a will, paying debts, and distributing assets. Costs vary by state (1–5% of estate value). Takes 6–18 months. Public record.

**Intestacy:** What happens when you die without a valid will. State law dictates distribution — typically spouse gets a share and children split the rest, but formulas vary.

**QTIP Trust:** Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust. Surviving spouse gets income for life; you choose the remainder beneficiaries (typically first-marriage children). Qualifies for the marital deduction.

**ILIT:** Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust. Owns the policy so proceeds are outside your taxable estate. Requires Crummey notices for annual premium gifts.

**GRAT:** Grantor Retained Annuity Trust. You transfer appreciating assets and retain an annuity for a term. If the assets outperform the IRS 7520 rate, the excess passes to beneficiaries gift-tax-free.

**SLAT:** Spousal Lifetime Access Trust. An irrevocable trust for the benefit of your spouse (and descendants). Removes assets from your estate while allowing indirect access through distributions to your spouse.

**Step-Up in Basis:** At death, the tax basis of assets is reset to fair market value. Heirs who sell inherited assets pay capital gains only on appreciation after the date of death — not appreciation during the decedent's lifetime.

**Portability / DSUE:** The surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's unused federal estate-tax exclusion — but only if Form 706 is filed. Combined exclusion can reach ~$30M for a married couple.

**GST Tax:** Generation-Skipping Transfer tax. A separate 40% tax on transfers to grandchildren or more remote descendants (or unrelated persons 37.5+ years younger). Each person has a GST exemption equal to the estate-tax exemption.
